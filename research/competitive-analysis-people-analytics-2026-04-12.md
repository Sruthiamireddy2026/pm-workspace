# People Analytics Market — Competitive Landscape Analysis
**Date of research:** 2026-04-12 | **Analyst note:** Based on training data through mid-2025. Verify pricing and recent product moves against live sources before sharing with stakeholders.

---

## Competitors Profiled

Visier · Workday People Analytics · SAP SuccessFactors Workforce Analytics · One Model · Lattice

---

## Competitor Profiles

### 1. Visier

**Positioning:** The definitive enterprise people analytics platform. Visier positions as the system of record for workforce intelligence — purpose-built for analytics, not bolted onto an HRIS.

**Target customer:** Large enterprises, 5,000+ employees. Buyers are CHROs, VP People Analytics, and HR ops leaders at companies sophisticated enough to have dedicated people analytics functions.

**Pricing:** Not publicly listed. Enterprise contracts typically start at $150k–$250k+/year. High implementation cost with professional services commonly required at launch.

**Strengths:**
- Deepest pre-built content library in the market — 2,000+ metrics and KPIs out of the box
- External workforce benchmarking data via Visier People network
- Strong workforce planning and headcount scenario modeling
- Attrition prediction and flight risk scoring at the employee level
- Purpose-built for people analytics (not a module inside a suite)

**Weaknesses:**
- Expensive and slow to implement — typical time-to-value is 3–6 months
- Requires a dedicated people analytics team to fully exploit; too complex for lean HR teams
- Limited flexibility for custom data models outside their schema
- Steep learning curve for non-analysts

**Recent moves:** Continued investment in AI-powered narrative generation ("Stories") and NLP query features. Expanded partner ecosystem with HRIS vendors.

---

### 2. Workday People Analytics

**Positioning:** Analytics built into your HCM. For Workday customers, the pitch is zero-ETL workforce intelligence that surfaces automated insights in plain language, without needing a data team.

**Target customer:** Existing Workday HCM customers, typically 1,000–50,000 employees. Buyers are HR leaders who want analytics without standing up a separate tool.

**Pricing:** Add-on to Workday HCM license. Pricing is bundled and negotiated; not independently purchasable.

**Strengths:**
- Native integration with Workday data — no data pipeline required
- ML-powered "Augmented Analytics" surfaces anomalies and stories automatically
- Executive-friendly: designed for CHRO consumption, not analyst consumption
- Single vendor, single contract for HRIS + analytics

**Weaknesses:**
- Hard dependency on Workday — no value for non-Workday customers, limited ability to blend external data sources
- Less powerful for advanced analysts who need custom models or raw data access
- Analytics capabilities lag pure-play vendors in depth and flexibility
- Roadmap tied to Workday's HCM release cycle

**Recent moves:** Pushed AI narrative generation and natural language Q&A features. Workday acquired HiredScore (AI for talent acquisition) in 2024, signaling deeper AI investment across the HCM suite.

---

### 3. SAP SuccessFactors Workforce Analytics

**Positioning:** Enterprise-grade HR reporting and compliance analytics for companies running SAP. Deeply integrated with SAP's broader ERP ecosystem.

**Target customer:** Large enterprises (10,000+ employees) already on SAP HCM or SuccessFactors. Strong in manufacturing, utilities, public sector, and regulated industries.

**Pricing:** Module pricing within SuccessFactors contracts. Not publicly disclosed; large enterprise deals.

**Strengths:**
- Deep integration with SAP ERP data (finance + HR in one view)
- Strong compliance and regulatory reporting (headcount, turnover, pay equity, labor law)
- Mature, battle-tested at global scale
- Broad localization for multinational companies

**Weaknesses:**
- Dated UX — widely criticized as complex and non-intuitive
- Predictive and ML capabilities significantly behind Visier and Workday
- Implementation is heavy; consulting-led deployments are the norm
- Limited appeal outside the SAP ecosystem
- Not a standalone product

**Recent moves:** SAP has been investing in Joule (their generative AI assistant) to surface conversational analytics across the SuccessFactors suite. Progress is incremental.

---

### 4. One Model

**Positioning:** The data integration-first people analytics platform. One Model's core bet is that HR data is fragmented across too many systems, and great analytics requires solving the data layer first.

**Target customer:** Mid-market to enterprise, 1,000–20,000 employees. Strong fit for companies with complex HR tech stacks (multiple HRIS, ATS, payroll, LMS systems) who are frustrated with siloed reporting.

**Pricing:** Mid-market pricing, more accessible than Visier. Typically $50k–$150k/year depending on size and scope.

**Strengths:**
- Broadest data integration library — connects to 150+ HR systems out of the box
- Flexible data modeling — customers can define their own metrics and schemas
- Works independently of any specific HRIS
- Strong professional services team helps customers build their data foundation

**Weaknesses:**
- Less pre-built analytical content than Visier; more setup required
- Less name recognition; harder to sell to risk-averse enterprise buyers
- Predictive/ML capabilities are growing but not yet market-leading
- Still a smaller team; product velocity can lag larger competitors

**Recent moves:** Continued investment in the data integration layer and expanded pre-built content library. Growing presence in mid-market accounts.

---

### 5. Lattice

**Positioning:** The people management platform. Lattice combines performance management, OKR tracking, engagement surveys, and compensation with a people analytics layer — marketed as the OS for people teams.

**Target customer:** Mid-market companies, 200–2,000 employees, with progressive people teams. Buyers are Chief People Officers and HR leaders at tech companies and high-growth businesses.

**Pricing:** Modular SaaS pricing. Analytics is included in higher-tier plans or available as an add-on. Rough range: $11–$17/person/month depending on modules.

**Strengths:**
- Strong brand in the mid-market people management space
- Analytics is tightly integrated with performance, engagement, and compensation data — no separate data pipeline
- Manager-friendly dashboards built for action, not just reporting
- Easy to deploy; time-to-value measured in days, not months
- Strong community and customer success reputation

**Weaknesses:**
- Analytics capabilities are shallow compared to pure-play vendors — designed for visibility, not deep analysis
- Not suitable for complex workforce planning or predictive modeling
- Dependent on Lattice as the system of record — limited integration with external HR data sources
- Losing some mid-market share to Culture Amp (engagement), Rippling (HCM), and others expanding into adjacent spaces

**Recent moves:** Lattice made significant news in mid-2024 when it announced and then walked back plans to integrate contractor AI agents into its people management platform after customer backlash. Refocused on core performance + analytics roadmap.

## Whitespace Analysis

### 1. The manager layer: analytics that drives action, not just reporting
Every player in this market produces dashboards for HR teams and executives. None of them have genuinely cracked the manager use case — giving front-line managers timely, specific, and actionable signals about their team (flight risk for a specific person, engagement drop in a specific team, span-of-control strain). Lattice comes closest but stops at visibility. The opportunity is analytics that embeds into manager workflows (Slack, email, calendar) rather than requiring managers to log into yet another tool.

**Who it affects:** Companies with 500+ employees where the manager layer is the execution point for retention and engagement strategy.

**Why incumbents haven't filled it:** Visier and One Model are built for analyst personas. Workday and SAP are oriented toward HR ops and executives. Lattice has the right persona but lacks the analytical depth.

---

### 2. Mid-market companies with fragmented HR stacks
The mid-market (200–2,000 employees) is underserved. Visier is too expensive. Workday and SAP require you to already be a customer. One Model requires technical setup. Lattice is accessible but analytically shallow. Companies in this band typically run 3–5 disconnected HR tools (an ATS, a payroll system, a separate HRIS, an engagement tool) and have no clean way to get a unified workforce view. The product that solves multi-source data integration at mid-market price points with fast time-to-value has significant room.

**Who it affects:** HR leaders at Series C to public companies without a dedicated data engineering team.

**Why incumbents haven't filled it:** Data integration is technically hard and not glamorous. Pure-play vendors (Visier, One Model) have priced for enterprise. Suite vendors don't serve customers outside their ecosystem.

---

### 3. Skills and workforce intelligence tied to business outcomes
Current people analytics is largely backward-looking (turnover, headcount, tenure). The next competitive frontier is skills intelligence — understanding what capabilities exist in the workforce today, what's needed for strategic priorities, and where the gaps are. No player in this market has connected skills data to business outcomes (revenue per employee, project delivery, customer satisfaction) in a meaningful way. Demand for this is accelerating as companies face AI-driven workforce transformation and need to make build/buy/reskill decisions with evidence.

**Who it affects:** CHROs and Heads of Talent at companies actively managing workforce transformation.

**Why incumbents haven't filled it:** Skills data is messy and non-standardized across companies. Connecting HR data to business outcome data requires breaking out of HR system silos — a hard integration and organizational problem.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Visier owns enterprise** but is expensive and slow. Vulnerable to faster-moving competitors on AI-native UX.
- **Workday and SAP are captive audiences** — strong within their ecosystems, irrelevant outside them. Their analytics ambitions are capped by their HCM install base.
- **One Model has the right architecture** (data integration-first) but lacks brand and pre-built depth.
- **Lattice is the accessible option** but analytically shallow. More a performance management tool with analytics attached than a true people analytics platform.
- **The market is bifurcated** between expensive enterprise tools with depth, and affordable mid-market tools with limited analytical power. The gap in the middle is real.
